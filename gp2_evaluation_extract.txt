The objectives of the system are as follows:
• To design and implement an anomaly detection model that utilizes
appropriate AI techniques to analyze telemetry data and identify
abnormal behavior.
• To establish a continual learning process using fine-tuning that enables
the model to adapt to new data and maintain a high level of detection
accuracy over time.
• To evaluate the system using metrics that reflect the model’s behavior
under both normal and non-normal conditions, including sensitivity
to deviations, stability in normal telemetry, and the false alarm rate
(FAR).
• To validate the effectiveness of the system using real, unlabeled telemetry
data, supported by synthetically modified scenarios that introduce
abnormal behavior for testing and evaluation.
• To enhance the system’s cybersecurity posture by applying security
practices and policies that support data protection, access control, and
the integrity of system components during operation.
1.3 System Contribution
The proposed system contributes to the advancement of intelligent cybersecurity
solutions for satellite operations. The novelty of this work lies in combining
AI-based anomaly detection with continual learning in a single adaptive system
tailored to satellite environments. Unlike traditional rule-based systems, the
proposed approach evolves over time, learning from operational feedback and
detected anomalies to enhance resilience against emerging threats.

More specialized detection architectures have also emerged. Wang et al.
[40], introduced a two stage approach combining a DDMN pre-cleaning filter
with an LSTM forecasting model. Applied to over 1.3 million samples of
real spacecraft telemetry collected over 2.5 years, their system achieved an
F1-score of 0.995, demonstrating the importance of robust preprocessing.
However, this model remains static and does not adapt to mission drift or
new operational patterns.
Zhan et al. [ 41], used a Seq2Seq GRU Autoencoder that identifies anomalies
using reconstruction error, computed as:

where larger deviations indicate abnormal behavior. GRU networks being
computationally lighter than LSTMs offer strong capability for learning
long-range dependencies and showed resilience to noise, missing data, and
irregular sampling, conditions commonly encountered in satellite telemetry.
One of the most influential contributions in the field is NASA’s Telemanom
framework by Hundman et al. [ 42], Telemanom uses stacked LSTM layers
to forecast expected telemetry behavior and applies a dynamic thresholding
method to detect both point anomalies and contextual deviations. Tested on
NASA SMAP and MSL datasets, Telemanom achieved the highest F0.5 score
among compared methods and established a baseline for prediction-based
anomaly detection. Its influence extends to many subsequent works, including
hybrid LSTM/GRU systems and adaptive-learning extensions.
The use of reconstruction-based objectives has further evolved to address the
instability of traditional detectors in noisy environments. By establishing
a behavioral baseline through an LSTM Autoencoder, anomalies can be
effectively identified by evaluating the reconstruction deviation between the
observed telemetryXand its reconstructed counterpart ˆX[43].
The effectiveness of hybrid architectures has been further demonstrated in
securing satellite-based positioning signals. Recent evaluations of sequential
LSTM-GRU hybrid models have shown superior performance in detecting
complex anomalies, such as GPS spoofing attacks, compared to Single-layer
[44]. By integrating both LSTM and GRU layers, the system can achieve high

Discussion.Spike and noise attacks are detected reliably at both thresholds
because these attack types introduce strong abrupt deviations in telemetry
behaviour. Such deviations generate large reconstruction and gradient errors,
making them highly separable from the learned normal telemetry distribution.
Consequently, the detector achieves very high recall and strong F 1 scores for
both attack categories across multiple operating points.
Freeze attacks reach near-perfect recall at the sweep optimum but experience
noticeable degradation at stricter thresholds. This behaviour suggests that
certain freeze windows partially resemble stable nominal telemetry behaviour,
particularly when the frozen values remain within historically normal operating
ranges. As a result, some freeze anomalies generate only moderate anomaly
scores and become increasingly difficult to detect under conservative thresholds.
Drift anomalies constitute the most challenging attack category in the
evaluation. Unlike abrupt attacks, drift anomalies evolve gradually over
time and therefore generate smaller short-term deviations from the learned
normal distribution. As a result, the anomaly score margin for drift windows
remains relatively narrow compared with spike and noise attacks.
Although the detector achieves perfect recall for drift at the Best-F1 threshold,
recall decreases substantially under conservative thresholds such as p99. This
limitation indicates that gradual long-term deviations remain more difficult for
reconstruction-based anomaly detection architectures operating on relatively
short temporal windows.
The per-attack evaluation therefore demonstrates that the proposed hybrid
architecture is particularly effective for detecting abrupt telemetry anomalies
while remaining comparatively less sensitive to low-amplitude gradual temporal
deviations. These findings are consistent with the behaviour expected from
reconstruction and prediction-based anomaly detection models.
Overall, the evaluation demonstrates that the proposed hybrid architecture
achieves strong robustness against abrupt telemetry anomalies while maintaining
competitive performance under highly imbalanced conditions. The results also
highlight the importance of adaptive threshold selection and motivate future
work on improved drift-sensitive modelling techniques and longer temporal
context integration.
